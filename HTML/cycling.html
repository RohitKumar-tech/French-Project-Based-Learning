History of Cycling in France

France boasts a rich and fascinating history with cycling, woven deeply into its social fabric and sporting culture. Here's a glimpse into this enduring relationship:

Early Days and Racing:

  • 1867: The first organized cycle race took place in Paris, followed by the first women's race in Bordeaux.
  • 1868: The "penny-farthing" emerged in the UK, but its popularity was short-lived in France.
  • 1880s: The arrival of the "safety bicycle" with its chained rear wheel reignited passion for cycling.
  • 1889: The Paris Exposition showcased the safety bicycle, leading to increased production by iconic French brands like Peugeot and Manufrance.
  • Late 19th century: Long-distance races gained traction, fueled by bicycle manufacturers seeking to promote their products.

Birth of a Legend: The Tour de France

  • 1903: The legendary Tour de France was born, driven by Henri Desgrange, a sports journalist, to boost newspaper sales. This grueling multi-stage race has become a global phenomenon, solidifying France's association with cycling.

La Tour De France

The Tour de France is an annual men's multiple-stage bicycle race primarily held in France. It's considered:

  • The oldest of the three Grand Tours (the others being the Giro d'Italia and the Vuelta a España).
  • Generally the most prestigious cycling race in the world.
  • The Tour de France is a grueling test of a cyclist's physical and mental endurance. It attracts the best riders in the world and is a major sporting event in France, drawing millions of spectators along the route.
  • First held: 1903
  • Stages: Typically 21, covering around 3,500 km (2,200 miles)
